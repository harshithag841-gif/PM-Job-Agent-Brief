My Personal AI Job Agent — a product in one page.

Why it exists, who it serves, what it does, and what it chose not to do. Written from a product lens, not a technical one.

Built by Harshitha Gangappa Role Senior PM, AI specialist Build window 7 days Running cost ₹0 / month
The one-liner

A personal AI agent that reads 8 job platforms every morning, filters against my real career context, and drops 50 ranked, genuinely relevant roles into my inbox — so I spend time applying, not searching.

01 · Problem

Generic AI job autopilots don't know me.

Tools like Job Copilot, LazyApply and Massive are built to serve everyone on earth. That means they optimise for breadth — not for whether a role actually fits the person using them. When I tried them, out of 20 "recommended" jobs, only 5 to 7 felt genuinely relevant. The rest were noise: wrong seniority, wrong domain, wrong stage of career.

The gap isn't geography. It isn't scraping coverage. It's personalisation. A generic autopilot can't know that I'm a Senior PM who specialises in AI, that I care about product over project, that I'd rather read one sharp shortlist than scroll a firehose. And because it doesn't know, it hedges. It recommends everything. And "everything" is the same as "nothing" when you have ninety minutes before the next meeting.

The failure mode isn't the tool being bad. It's the tool being generic. A human recruiter who knew me for a day would outperform it. The ambition here is to give myself that recruiter — in software.

02 · The user

One person. By design.

This product has a user count of one. That's not a limitation — it's the whole point. Building for one real person, with a real resume and a real calendar, is what unlocks the personalisation that generic tools can't touch.

The senior specialist, mid-search
Archetype · transferable to any specialist IC / leader actively exploring roles
  • Has a tight point of view on what they want next — and what they don't.
  • Can tell a great job from a mediocre one in three seconds, but can't afford to read 200 of them.
  • Doesn't want to auto-apply. Wants to apply deliberately, with context.
  • Values their morning attention more than their money.
03 · The experience

What the morning looks like.

The product is consumed in under five minutes a day. No dashboard to log in to, no feed to scroll, no configuration to fiddle with. Just an email that knows what it's talking about.

1

The inbox ping at 08:15

A digest arrives every morning with a one-line summary and a clean spreadsheet attached.

2

Fifty roles, already ranked

The best-fit roles sit at the top. Each row carries a fit score, a freshness date, and the source platform.

3

Nothing I've seen before

Yesterday's roles never reappear. If a role is on the list, it's new to me today.

4

One click, I'm applying

Every row is a direct apply link. No intermediate landing page, no "sign in to see more."

04 · Why this is different

Personalisation, not aggregation.

Most job tools compete on how many listings they can pile up. This one competes on how many it can confidently throw away. The job of the agent is to behave like a careful editor, not an eager aggregator.

Generic AI autopilots

  • Built for every user on the planet.
  • Optimise for volume of matches.
  • Ranking is broad and shallow.
  • Auto-apply to inflate numbers.
  • Noise rate: ~65–75% of results.

This agent

  • Built for exactly one real person.
  • Optimises for signal over volume.
  • Ranking uses the user's actual resume and preferences.
  • Human applies, agent curates.
  • Noise rate: ~30% and dropping.

06 · Scope

What's in, what's deliberately out.

A product is defined as much by what it refuses to do as by what it does. These lines were drawn on day one and held through seven days of building.

In scope

  • Reading across multiple job platforms every day
  • Filtering out stale, irrelevant, or out-of-context roles
  • Scoring and ranking against the user's real resume and preferences
  • Remembering what was seen before, so nothing repeats
  • Delivering a clean, sortable shortlist by email

Out of scope

  • Auto-applying on the user's behalf
  • Writing cover letters or editing resumes
  • Impersonating the user in recruiter conversations
  • Replacing the judgement of the person searching
  • Scaling to many users — this is a personal tool, not a platform
07 · Where it's going

The next few product bets.

Shipping v1 is the easy part. The interesting product questions start now — around how much context the agent is allowed to carry, and how honest it's allowed to be.

Shipped

v1 — The daily shortlist

Eight platforms read, fifty roles ranked, zero duplicates, one email. Runs on its own every morning.

Next

v2 — Feedback that actually changes tomorrow

A one-tap "not for me" signal that shapes the next day's ranking. The agent should learn from what I skip, not just what I apply to.

Next

v2 — An honest "why this role" note

Each top role shows a short, plain-English reason it made the cut. If the reason is weak, the rank should be weak.

Later

v3 — Pattern surfacing across weeks

Once there's enough history, call out the quiet trends — which companies keep showing up, which role titles are drifting, where the market is actually hiring.
